2  Introduction

The commodification of education has been one of the most significant transformations in the organization of welfare states over the past few decades. Although education has traditionally been a social service guaranteed by the state, many countries have undergone a process of commodification that has led to a relative decline in public funding and an expansion of private education provision (Busemeyer & Iversen, 2020; Esping-Andersen, 1985; Lindh, 2015; OECD, 2025). This shift toward market logic has not only transformed the institutional functioning of education systems but also people’s beliefs and preferences regarding how educational opportunities should be distributed, demonstrating the impact of economic organization on micro-level dynamics (Busemeyer, 2012; Busemeyer et al., 2018; Koos & Sachweh, 2019). In some cases, these beliefs have led to the legitimization of educational inequality (Castillo, Iturra, et al., 2025; Piketty & Sandel, 2025)

Meritocracy holds that social rewards should be distributed on the basis of individual effort and talent, making it one of the main normative principles associated with both education and distributional preferences (Sandel, 2020). Various studies have shown that meritocratic beliefs are linked to higher acceptance of inequality, as they attribute success and failure primarily to individual responsibility, functioning as a cultural mechanism that justifies social hierarchies and diminishes perceptions of structural factors that shape individual trajectories (Darnon et al., 2018; Heuer et al., 2020; Mau, 2015; Mijs, 2016; Mijs, 2021). However, the understanding of meritocratic beliefs has evolved in recent years, no longer being viewed as a one-dimensional phenomenon, but rather as capable of coexisting with other beliefs that acknowledge structural conditions and privilege, thereby overcoming a methodological and theoretical limitation (Castillo et al., 2023; Zhu, 2025). Within the multidimensional framework of meritocracy, some studies have proposed a distinction between meritocratic perceptions and structural perceptions (Zhu, 2025); others have focused on descriptive and ascriptive dimensions of effort, talent, and privilege (Castillo et al., 2023; Duru-Bellat & Tenret, 2012; Sin et al., 2026).

Several studies have examined the effect of education on the justification of inequalities (Liu & Wang, 2026; Nye et al., 2026), or the relationship between meritocracy and perceptions of inequality (Bartram, 2023; Castillo, Iturra, et al., 2025; Mijs, 2021), but to our knowledge, no research has examined market preferences in education and their relationship to meritocratic beliefs from a multidimensional and comparative perspective. To fill this gap, this study aims to analyze the role of meritocratic beliefs in preferences for market justice in education and their interaction with institutional contexts. The concept of market justice refers to the belief that access to and the quality of social services should be determined by individual or family ability to pay, and is thus associated with the legitimization of inequalities from a market-oriented perspective. (Lane, 1986; Lindh, 2015).

This study aims to answer three questions: (1) To what extent do meritocratic beliefs influence preferences for market justice in education? (2) How do institutional designs and preferences for market justice in education interact? (3) Are there significant differences across countries? We argue that preferences for market justice in education are closely related to two factors: meritocratic beliefs and a country’s institutional designs. On the one hand, meritocratic beliefs have been shown to be a determinant of distributional preferences and perceptions of inequality, where those who perceive more meritocracy are likely to be more inclined to prefer market justice in education [; Castillo et al. (2026); Castillo, Laffert, et al. (2025); Castillo et al. (2024); Darnon et al. (2018); Heuer et al. (2020); Mijs (2021)]. On the other hand, countries’ institutional designs appear to be an important factor in shaping people’s beliefs (Busemeyer, 2013; Liu & Wang, 2026), and we hypothesize that countries with higher levels of economic inequality, as well as those with greater public investment in education, will exhibit an aversion to market justice in education.

To address these issues, we use data from the 2019 International Social Survey Program (ISSP), the World Inequality Database (WID), and UNESCO. First, we apply latent class analysis to identify profiles of meritocratic beliefs, and then conduct multinomial logistic regression to examine the relationship between these profiles and preferences for market justice in education, while incorporating sociodemographic and institutional factors. In doing so, this research aims to contribute to the literature on the relationship between perceptions of inequality and meritocracy, placing special emphasis on micro-level foundations and their interaction with institutional contexts to understand how beliefs about educational inequality are formed.
